What Are Exoplanets?

Exoplanets are planets beyond our solar system. Some orbit stars like our Sun, while others drift freely in space. With over 5,600 confirmed exoplanets, and many more waiting to be discovered, exoplanets come in all shapes and sizes!

How Do We Discover Them?

Astronomers have found exoplanets using methods like:

  • Transit Method: Observing the dimming of a star as a planet crosses in front of it.
  • Radial Velocity: Measuring tiny movements in a star due to the gravitational tug of a planet.
  • Direct Imaging: Capturing images of planets by masking out the light of their parent stars.

Famous Exoplanets

Proxima Centauri b

The closest exoplanet to Earth, just 4.2 light-years away. It might be rocky and possibly within its star's habitable zone!

Proxima Centauri b

Proxima Centauri b is an exoplanet located approximately 4.2 light-years from Earth, making it the closest known exoplanet to our solar system. It orbits within the habitable zone of its star, Proxima Centauri, where conditions may allow for liquid water to exist, a crucial element for life as we know it. The planet is roughly 1.17 times the size of Earth and is believed to be rocky in nature. Proxima Centauri is a red dwarf star, smaller and cooler than our Sun, which impacts the planet's climate. Notably, Proxima Centauri b experiences tidal locking, meaning one side of the planet always faces its star, leading to extreme temperature differences between the two sides.

Kepler-452b

Kepler-452b is located about 1,400 light-years away in the constellation Cygnus and is often referred to as "Earth's cousin" due to its Earth-like characteristics. The planet is approximately 60% larger than Earth and resides in the habitable zone of its star, Kepler-452, which is similar to our Sun. This proximity to a sun-like star enhances the potential for Earth-like conditions, making it a prime candidate for habitability. Kepler-452b has an orbital period of about 385 days, resulting in a longer year than Earth's. Scientists speculate that it could have a rocky surface and possibly contain water, further bolstering its habitability prospects.

Kepler-186f

Known for being the first Earth-sized planet in the habitable zone, increasing hopes for potential life.

Kepler-186f

Kepler-186f is an intriguing exoplanet located about 500 light-years away in the constellation Cygnus. It holds a significant place in the search for extraterrestrial life as it was the first Earth-sized planet discovered in the habitable zone of another star. The planet is about 1.1 times the size of Earth, suggesting a similar composition, which raises hopes for the presence of life-sustaining conditions. Kepler-186f orbits its star, Kepler-186, every 130 days and receives about one-third the sunlight that Earth does. This positioning allows for the potential presence of liquid water on its surface, making it an exciting target for future research and exploration.

Trappist-1e

Part of the Trappist-1 system, a rocky planet with Earth-like conditions, located 40 light-years away.

Trappist-1e

Trappist-1e is one of the seven Earth-sized exoplanets orbiting the ultra-cool dwarf star Trappist-1, located about 40 light-years away. Among its planetary siblings, Trappist-1e is considered to be in the habitable zone, where conditions may be suitable for liquid water. This exoplanet has a similar size to Earth, with a rocky composition that could potentially support life. Trappist-1e experiences a short orbital period of about 6.1 days, suggesting that it could have a climate akin to Earth's. The star's dim light allows for more stable temperatures on the planet, further enhancing the possibility of a habitable environment. This makes Trappist-1e an exciting candidate for ongoing astronomical studies and the search for extraterrestrial life.
